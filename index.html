🔧 How Engines Work — The 4-Stroke Cycle

Every gasoline car engine follows four steps to turn fuel into motion:

1. Intake: The piston goes down, sucking in air and fuel mixture.
2. Compression: The piston goes up, squeezing (compressing) the mixture.
3. Power: The spark plug ignites the mixture — BOOM — pushing the piston down hard. This is what creates motion.
4. Exhaust: The piston goes back up, pushing out the burned gas through the exhaust.

This happens thousands of times per minute in every cylinder. Most cars have 4 or 6 cylinders working together.

Why it matters: Understanding this cycle helps you diagnose issues like misfires, rough idle, or lack of power.

LESSON 02
Basic Car Systems
Brakes, cooling, electrical, fuel — the main systems in every vehicle.
● Locked

🔧 The 5 Main Car Systems

1. Engine System: Converts fuel to motion (what you just learned).
2. Cooling System: Keeps the engine from overheating. Uses coolant, radiator, water pump, and thermostat.
3. Braking System: Pads press against rotors to slow the wheels. Brake fluid transfers pressure from your foot to the brakes.
4. Electrical System: Battery starts the car. Alternator charges the battery while driving. Powers lights, radio, etc.
5. Fuel System: Tank → Fuel pump → Fuel filter → Injectors → Engine. Delivers the right amount of fuel.

Every car problem falls into one of these systems. Learn them and you can diagnose almost anything.

LESSON 03
Tools Every Mechanic Needs
The essential tools for starting any repair work.

🔧 Essential Mechanic Tools

Socket Set: The #1 most used tool. Gets bolts on and off. Get metric AND standard.
Wrench Set: For when you can't fit a socket. Open-end and box-end.
Screwdrivers: Phillips and flat-head in multiple sizes.
Pliers: Needle-nose, regular, and channel-lock.
Jack & Jack Stands: NEVER go under a car held up only by a jack. Stands save lives.
OBD2 Scanner: Plugs into your car and reads error codes. Under $30 and incredibly useful.

Start small. You don't need everything — build your collection over time.

Understand short-term memory in a computer and why it matters.
● Start

💻 What is RAM?

RAM stands for Random Access Memory. Think of it as your computer's short-term memory.

Analogy: Your desk is RAM. The bigger your desk, the more papers (programs) you can work on at once. Your filing cabinet is your hard drive — it stores everything long-term.

When you open Chrome, Spotify, and a game — all of those sit in RAM. When RAM fills up, your computer slows down.

Key facts:
• RAM is volatile — it loses data when powered off
• Most PCs have 8GB-16GB of RAM
• RAM comes in sticks that plug into the motherboard
• More RAM = more multitasking power
LESSON 02
How Does a Computer Boot?
What happens from the moment you press the power button.
● Locked

💻 The Boot Process

When you press the power button, here's what happens in order:

1. Power Supply activates: Sends electricity to the motherboard.
2. BIOS/UEFI runs: The first software that runs. It's built into the motherboard. It checks that your hardware is working (called POST — Power On Self Test).
3. Boot device found: BIOS looks for a drive with an operating system (usually your SSD or HDD).
4. OS loads: Windows, Linux, or macOS starts loading from the drive into RAM.
5. Login screen: OS is ready. You're in.

If boot fails: The issue is usually in steps 1-3 — bad power supply, failed RAM, or missing boot drive.

LESSON 03
What is an IP Address?
How computers find each other on a network.
● Locked

💻 IP Addresses Explained

Every device on a network gets a unique address — just like every house has a mailing address.

IP Address = Internet Protocol Address
Example: 192.168.1.15
Private IP: Used inside your home network. Your router assigns these. Starts with 192.168.x.x or 10.x.x.x
Public IP: Your address on the internet. Given by your ISP. This is what websites see.
How to find yours:
• Windows: Open cmd → type ipconfig
• Public: Google "what is my IP"

Understanding IPs is foundational for networking, troubleshooting, and cybersecurity.

The big picture — what it is, why it matters, and where you fit in.

🔐 What is Cybersecurity?

Cybersecurity is the practice of protecting computers, networks, and data from unauthorized access, attacks, or damage.

Think of it this way: Every company, government, and person has digital information. Cybersecurity professionals are the ones who keep it safe.

There are different roles in cybersecurity:

🔴 Red Team (Offensive): Ethical hackers who try to break into systems to find weaknesses.
🔵 Blue Team (Defensive): People who monitor, detect, and respond to attacks.
🟣 Purple Team: Combination of both — tests AND defends.

Why it's hot right now: There are millions of unfilled cybersecurity jobs. Companies are desperate for skilled people. You don't need a degree — certifications and skills matter most.

LESSON 02
Types of Cyber Attacks
Phishing, malware, ransomware — know the threats.
● Locked

🔐 Common Cyber Attacks

Phishing: Fake emails or messages that trick you into giving up passwords or clicking malicious links. #1 most common attack.
Malware: Malicious software — viruses, trojans, worms. Gets on your system through downloads or links.
Ransomware: Locks your files and demands payment to unlock them. Devastating for businesses.
Social Engineering: Manipulating people (not computers) to get access. "Hey, I'm from IT, what's your password?"
Brute Force: Trying millions of password combinations until one works.

Knowing these attacks is the first step to defending against them — or ethically testing for them.

LESSON 03
Networking Basics for Security
You can't secure a network if you don't understand how it works.
● Locked

🔐 Networking Foundations

Before you can hack or defend anything, you need to understand how data moves.

Packets: All data on the internet is broken into small pieces called packets. They travel from your device to the server and back.
Ports: Like doors on a server. Port 80 = HTTP (web). Port 443 = HTTPS (secure web). Port 22 = SSH. Hackers scan for open ports.
Firewalls: Software or hardware that blocks unauthorized traffic. Like a bouncer at a club door.
DNS: Translates domain names (google.com) into IP addresses (142.250.80.14). The "phone book" of the internet.

This is foundational. Every cybersecurity tool and technique builds on understanding networks.

🛟 Support Guide

When She's Stressed

  • Listen first. Don't try to fix it immediately.
  • Ask: "Do you want me to listen or help solve this?"
  • Be calm. Your energy affects hers.
  • Small gestures > big words.

How to Respond, Not React

  • Pause before speaking. 5-second rule.
  • Ask yourself: "Will this help or hurt?"
  • If heated, say: "I need a minute, I'm not leaving."
  • Come back calmer. Always come back.

Staying Consistent

  • Show up even on easy days.
  • Consistency builds trust more than grand gestures.
  • Track your effort — not to prove it, but to stay aware.
  • Be reliable in small things.
